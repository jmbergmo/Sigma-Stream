# Scientific Skincare: DOE for the Perfect Glow

## The Quest for Consistency: Beyond the Bottle

We've all been there: staring a bathroom shelf full of "miracle" products, yet waking up with skin that decides to behave randomly. One day it's glowing, the next it's dull or breaking out. The beauty industry tells us the solution is always *more*: more products, more expensive ingredients, more steps.

But what if the secret isn't *what* you use, but *how* you use it?

## Step 1: Defining the Factors

We stripped the routine down to its essentials and identified four key variables (factors) that are often overlooked or inconsistent. We set a "Low" and "High" level for each to test the extremes.

*   **A: Water Temperature:** Cool vs. Warm
*   **B: Cleanser Amount:** Pea-sized vs. Coin-sized
*   **C: Scrub Duration:** 15 seconds vs. 60 seconds
*   **D: Towel Dry Method:** Pat Dry vs. Rub Dry

## Step 2: The Experiment (L8 Array)

Instead of trying every possible combination (which would take weeks and introduce too much environmental variation), we used Sigma-Stream to generate a streamlined **L8 Orthogonal Array**. This allowed us to test all four factors efficiently in just 8 nights.

Our response variable (the metric for success) was a "Morning Glow Score" (1-10), assessed blindly each morning based on texture, redness, and radiance.

## Step 3: Analysis and Results

The results were surprising. Sigma-Stream analyzed the data to determine which factors actually moved the needle.

### The Pareto Principle in Action

The Pareto chart revealed the heavy hitters. We expected 'Cleanser Amount' to be key (more soap = cleaner, right?), but the data told a different story.

![Pareto Chart of Skincare Factors](/blog/assets/skincare-pareto.png)

*   **Water Temperature (Factor A):** The single most critical factor. Warm water consistently led to better absorption and texture scores compared to cool water.
*   **Scrub Duration (Factor C):** The second most important variable. 60 seconds proved significantly better than 15, confirming the "60-second rule" often cited by dermatologists.
*   **Cleanser Amount & Towel Dry:** Surprisingly, these had negligible impact on the final glow score. You can save your expensive cleanser!

### Hidden Interactions

Crucially, DOE uncovered a hidden interaction that simple trial-and-error would have missed.

![Interaction Plot](/blog/assets/skincare-interaction.png)

There was a strong interaction between **Scrub Duration** and **Cleanser Amount**.
*   When using a **Pea-sized** amount, the **60-second** scrub was essential effectively work the product in.
*   When using a **Coin-sized** amount, the duration mattered less, but it was wasteful.

This insight allows for an optimized, efficient routine: Use a small amount of product (saving money) but take the time to massage it in (investing time).

## Conclusion: Optimized Radiance

By applying the Six Sigma DFFS (Design for Six Sigma) approach, we turned a guessing game into a repeatable science.
